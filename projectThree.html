Analyzing Visual Media: “Stacy’s Mom” by Fountains of Wayne



The way media has portrayed women and children has changed greatly over the last few decades. Advertisements used to be able to display images of vulnerable, sexualized, and beaten women in order to sell products. The 2009 music video “Stacy’s Mom,” which is by Fountains of Wayne, would be very controversial today since it demonstrates these old marketing tactics. This video communicates a disturbing message about women (especially mothers) to the band’s audience. The band uses imagery that is very prevalent in our culture, such as women being the object of men’s fantasies and younger boys ogling older women. The Fountains of Wayne's music video, “Stacy’s Mom,” completely disregards the traditional mother-child cultural code by not only fetishizing the mother in an extremely sexual way but by also using a child to convey that message.




In the music video “Stacy’s Mom,” we see a young woman who is believed to be Stacy’s mom. We see her picking up a girl, whom we assume to be Stacy, from a crosswalk with a bunch of teenage boys. It then transitions to Stacy on her bed in her swimsuit, followed by her going outside to a pool to meet a boy. The boy is lounging in the pool watching the mother get undressed, while he completely ignores Stacy. The next scene depicts the boy mowing a lawn while Stacy is lounging on a chair watching him. Stacy’s mom then comes out in only a towel and gets a massage with the boy ogling her. The following scene is the boy and Stacy sitting on the couch together watching TV when the mother comes out to clean. The boy then proceeds to stare at her chest while she cleans. The following scene then shows the mom doing a stripper show in fishnets and leather (likely imagined by the boy). The final scene shows the boy staring at the mother in the pool, looking happy with his eyes closed. The video ends with the daughter walking in on the boy... doing something, and immediately leaving the room with a shocked look on her face.



The major issue within this video is the fact that it breaks the traditional mother-child cultural code by showing a teenage boy ogling his friend’s mother. There are multiple situations within this video where the boy is supposed to be hanging out with Stacy, but instead he stares at her mother and ignores Stacy. For example, in one scene the boy and Stacy are watching something on the TV when Stacy’s mom comes out to clean the kitchen. The boy then turns around to watch the mother clean, which shows that he is consciously trying to watch the mother. The lyrics reinforce this idea with lines, such as “Stacy, can't you see? You're just not the girl for me/I know it might be wrong, but I'm in love with Stacy's mom” (Fountains of Wayne lines 13-14). This shows that he is more interested in the “adult woman” than in kids his age. This music video parallels a Diet Pepsi ad described in Jean Kilbourne’s “‘Two Ways a Woman Can Get Hurt’: Advertising and Violence” where she describes the commercial as “Cindy Crawford being ogled by two boys (they seemed to be about twelve years old) as she got out of her car and bought a Pepsi from a machine” (559). The similarity is young boys gawking over older women. Kilbourne argues that these sorts of commercials are dangerous because they are already exposing young boys to the sexist dynamic of men and women. In a way, they are communicating that it is okay for men to look at women in a sexual (or sexist) manner.


Another aspect that shows this inappropriate relationship would be the sexual innuendos. One of the innuendos depicts the mother undressing (which is already inappropriate) while the boy opens an erupting bottle of soda. This soda is representing a sexual activity, which is why it is inappropriate to communicate that imagery with a kid. Kilbourne discusses how young boys are described in media, stating that “many boys grow up feeling that they are unmanly if they are not always ‘ready for action,’ capable of and interested in sex with any woman who is available” (563). In accordance with our cultural codes, most people agree that twelve-year-old boys are not and should not be “ready for action.” They should not be getting any “action” with peers or otherwise. These kinds of topics are not be the “norm” for kids; they are goofing off and having fun with their childhood, not trying to have sex.


The other major issue with the video is the objectification of the “Stacy’s Mom” character. There are numerous scenes in this video when the camera angles are displaying her in a very sexual light. For example, there is a scene in the video where "Stacy’s Mom” pole dances in the boy’s imagination. This is very disturbing because that means that at his age, he is already objectifying her (not thinking about her as his friend’s mother but a sexual object). As Kilbourne puts it, “Sex in advertising is pornographic because it dehumanizes and objectifies people, especially women, and because it fetishizes products, imbues them with an erotic charge” (546). While the video is not an advertisement, it imbues the band’s music with that erotic charge. With their sexual portrayal of “Stacy’s Mom,” they send a message to their audience about their music. This message may be interpreted simply as she is a hot mom or as horrifyingly as women are nothing but sexual objects, depending on who is listening.



Fountains of Wayne defy the traditional mother-child cultural code in a the most inappropriate way possible. I would fear the ideas and messages within this video that a child could take away. Not only are they showing this one-sided love interest in a mother, but they also blatantly objectify her multiple times. However, there is another side to this story. The story is a slice of life about a teenage boy going through puberty and seeing his friend’s mom as attractive (Ezzo). The bassist and songwriter Adam Schlesinger said in an interview with SongFacts that “[O]ne of my best friends told me that he thought my grandmother was really hot, that added a little bit to the song. That’s a true story. And my grandmother was pretty hot.” With this being the original meaning behind the band’s song, does that mean that the video is okay? Their intentions behind the video do not change the fact the video is fundamentally wrong with its portrayal of women and children.
